Observation of Study Behaviours at the University of Auckland General Library – A Visual Story

Visualization 1

A bar chart that shows the proportion of focused mood highest in early morning and early afternoon.

This visualization plot aims to analyze the relationship between the time of the day and the proportion of focused study mood/study condition among students.

To begin with, I was collecting data 3 times a day and categorized the categorical variable time_of_the_day as Morning if the Timestamp was between 8:00 - 11:00 a.m., Noon if the Timestamp was between 11:30-14:30, and Late Afternoon if it was between 15:30 -18:30. And during these observation periods, I observed students’ general behaviors in level M and level 2 of the library, and categorized their condition of studying as either focused, energetic, tired or distracted, into a variable called general_mood. I was interested to find out the relationship between the state of focus and the time_of_the_day, so I calculated the focused situation grouped by the time_of_the_day. I did this first by using str_detect() to select the data points that were in focused situation for the variable general_mood, and grouped the data by time_of_the_day using function group_by(). Then, I created a logical binary variable called is_focused that counts TRUE if the situation is focused, and FALSE otherwise.Furthermore, I summarized the mean over this logical vector to count the proportion that it is TRUE(the proportion of “focused”). Finally, I created the bar charts using geom_col() to analyze the relationship between time_of_the day(x axis) the proportion_of_focused(y axis).

The result was compelling. Firstly, the proportion of focused mood was 100% in the morning, which was substantially higher than in all other time periods.

This suggests that concentration levels peak earlier in the day, possibly due to fewer distractions or the freshness of starting work early.

By noon, the proportion of focused situation dropped to around 30%, and by late afternoon, it was near 0.

This shows a clear downward trend in focus as the day progresses, which may be due to fatigue, cognitive overload, or socializing and multitasking.

Visualization 2

A dot plot that shows group study ratio is relatively low during the quietest time slots

This plot aims to observe whether there is a relationship between the group study ratio and the noise level of the library, and especially how the level of group study behaves under relatively quiet environment.

To begin with, the level of quietness is measured by the noise level from 1 to 5, and the smaller the number the quieter the environment. Noticeably, this number combines both perceived feelings and objective measurements. This is because some of my data(from March 31th to April 2nd) were recalled to incorporate the uniqueness of exam periods, which may be of great importance for my analysis in the context of student behaviors in the library. For these days, noise level was a subjective ranking from 1(silent), 2(very quiet), 3(moderate) to 5(noisy and distracting). For my actually recorded dates, my noise level was an objective measurement of noise level in dBs using my iphone APP. I categorized levels as 1(30-35 db), level2(36-45 dB), level 3(46-55 dB), level 4(56-65 dB) and level 5(66-75 dB).

Then I used the arrange() function to sort the data according to the noise level(from quiet to loud), and selected the top 12 most quiet data points using slice(). Afterwards I used geom_point() to plot these top 12 quiet data points with timestamp as x axis and the group study ratio as the y axis, which is a clear and convenient way to see the group study ratio for these “quiet” time points.

There are 2 main observations: 1)While group study was present during the quietest time slots, the ratios remained generally ≤ 20%, which was substantially lower than the overall observed range of group study ratio (up to 70–80%).

This suggests that while students may still engage in collaborative work in quiet environments, these interactions were likely smaller in scale or more quiet in nature, rather than large, active group sessions.

2)Group study ratios range from 0% to 20%, showing considerable behavioral diversity even in similarly quiet settings. This suggests that noise level itself did not predict how collaborative students were — other factors may influence the extent of group interactions.

Visualization 3

A scatter plot comparing noise level and stress signs: stress is higher in exam periods with increased noise.

This plot aims to analyze the relationship between noise_level and visible stress signs with a focus to distinguish between exam and non-exam periods. It is analyzing whether students are more or less stressed when the noise levels are different, and whether this pattern changes during exam or non-exam periods.

To begin with, the variable visible_stress_signs is a logical variable that counts Yes if I saw or recorded evident signs of stress among students, like pacing, hair-scratching, head-holding and intense typing, and no otherwise. And the exam_period variable is TRUE for my recalled period of data(March 31 to April 2), and FALSE for any collection afterwards that are actual records.

I plotted the noise_level(x axis) over the visible stress signs(y axis), with different colors using scale_colour_manual to distinguish between exam and non-exam periods. I plotted this using geom_jitter() so that I can avoid overlapping of the dots, which is necessary here since the variable of the y-axis only has 2 categories, and so it is easy for the data points to stack together and cover up the pattern.

The two main findings are as follows.

Firstly, visible stress signs were more frequent during exam periods.Despite having fewer total exam-period observations (pink points), a relatively larger proportion of them were at “Yes” for Visible_Stress_Signs. This suggests that students were more visibly stressed during exams.

Secondly, even if there was no deterministic relationship between noise level and visible stress signs, students appeared more stressed when the noise level was ≤ 4 (i.e., moderately quiet environments). A possible explanation for this was the influence of a confounding variable: Main Activities. Students in quieter environments were more likely to be studying alone, while those in noisier environments might be engaged in group studies or chatting, which were typically more social, less stressful and less cognitively demanding.
